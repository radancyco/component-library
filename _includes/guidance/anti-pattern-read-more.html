Why Is This an Anti-Pattern?

Disclosures should generally open content below them, not above. When we stray from this, we break the expectation users have of downward vertical scrolling and interaction and also risk breaking accessibility for certain user groups.

Keyboard Users

When a disclosure is expanded, a keyboard users expectation, particularly a blind user, is that the next tab stop will bring them to the open content below the button they just engaged with. When we open the content above the button, the next forward tab stop will not bring them to this content, nor may the user be aware that content is above the button and that they will have to now tab backwards to access it. To manage this issue, we need to move focus to the open content, but in doing so, we now create a new issue which poses a problem for other user groups.

Screen Magnification Users

Screen magnifiers are an assistive technology that people with low vision use to zoom in on the page. By moving focus, the experience for screen magnifier users may be confusing because they are now no longer zoomed in on the section that they were, causing them to become lost on the page. This is why moving focus should be done sparingly on the web.

Neurodiverse Users

Shifting focus to open content may also be an issue for neurodiverse users, who may find it equally distracting and confusing.

Why is this an Anti-Pattern?

Disclosure buttons should generally open content below them, not above. When we stray from this best practice, we break the expectation users have of downward vertical scrolling and interaction and risk breaking accessibility for certain user groups.

Keyboard Users

When a disclosure is expanded, a keyboard users' expectation, particularly a blind or low-vision user, is that the next tab stop will bring them to the open content below the button they have just engaged with. When we open the content above the button, the next forward tab stop will not bring them to this content, nor may the user be aware that the content is above the button and that they should tab backwards to access it. To manage this issue, we need to programmatically move focus to the open content, but in doing so, we now create a new issue which poses a problem for other user groups.

Screen Magnification Users

Screen magnifiers are an assistive technology that people with low vision use to zoom in on the page. By moving focus, the experience for screen magnifier users may become confusing because they are now no longer zoomed in on the section that they were just at, causing them to become lost on the page. In addition to moving focus, the natural shifting of the page when the disclosure is closed, will also cause a user to lose their place.

Neuro-diverse Users

Unexpectedly shifting focus to open content may also be an issue for neuro-diverse users, who may find it equally distracting and confusing.

Solution

While moving focus on the web is sometimes necessary, it should be done sparingly. Instead, embrace common paradigms for revealing content so that everybody using the interface, regardless of the tools they use to access it, can have a positive and pleasant experience.

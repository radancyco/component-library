Disclosure buttons are generally more user-friendly when they reveal content below them rather than above. When we stray from this best practice, we disrupt the natural user expectation of downward vertical scrolling and interaction, and compromise accessibility for specific user groups.

Keyboard Users

When a disclosure is expanded, keyboard users, especially those who are blind or have low vision, expect that the next tab will lead them to the open content below the button they've just engaged with. When content opens above the button, the next forward tab will not bring them to this content, and the user might not be aware that the content is located above the button. If they do notice it, then they will need to tab backwards to access it. To address this issue, we might consider programmatically shifting focus to the open content, but this approach introduces a new problem impacting other user groups.

Screen Magnification Users

Screen magnifiers are assistive tools used by individuals with low vision to zoom in on web pages. Shifting focus can be disorienting for screen magnifier users because they are no longer zoomed in on the section they were just viewing, potentially causing them to lose their place on the page. Additionally, the natural shifting of the page when the disclosure closes can also disrupt the user's orientation.

Neurodivergent Users

Unexpectedly shifting focus to open content may also pose challenges for neurodivergent users, who may find it equally distracting and confusing.

Solution

While there are situations where moving focus may be necessary, it should be employed sparingly. It's more beneficial to adhere to common paradigms for revealing content so that all users interacting with the interface, regardless of the tools they use, can enjoy a positive and seamless experience.
